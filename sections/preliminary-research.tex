\subsection{Federated learning} \label{subsec:federative-learning}
Imagine there are researchers working on a project and they do not tell each other anything about themselves, their names, education, age\ldots They only contribute with their current knowledge without revealing their personal information. This is what federated learning tries to achieve~\cite{FederatedLearningCollaborativea}. Later on I talk about global and local models, in this example, researchers play a role of local models, and the project would be a global model.

Federated learning is a \emph{decentralized learning} technique, which means the learning is not accomplished centrally on a single device --- normally a server --- but is kept on the edge, close to the main source of the data. Since it is decentralized, this also leads to great benefits regarding \emph{privacy} and regulations as data is not moved out of its safe zone. In practice, we accomplish this using two types artificial intelligence models: global and local. The algorithm~\cite{strykerWhatFederatedLearning2025} contains the following steps:

\begin{enumerate}
 \item Global model is initialized.
 \item Global model sends a copy of himself to all the local models. In a medical example, local models would be hospitals.
 \item Local models train on sensitive (medical) data. There is no data exchanged between \emph{any} of the models.
 \item Local models send the weights to the global model. One can image they share their knowledge, without data.
 \item Global model combines the weights --- usually by averaging them ---, and becomes better at predicting diseases, for example.
 \item The process repeats from the second step. Each local model now becomes better as it receives the average ``knowledge'' from all others.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Types}
There are two main types of federated learning that can be implemented based on the similarities between datasets of clients. A definition of a dataset is essential: A \emph{dataset} is a collection of data, commonly structured into a format comparable to a spreadsheet where each rows represent a record (also called example). A record consists of values that represent either a feature or a label. A feature is input for a machine learning model, such as heart rate. A label is either output of a machine learning model --- in a disease prediction examples would be 0 (``Obstructive sleep apnea not detected'') or 1 (``Obstructive sleep apnea detected'') --- or an answer for a machine learning model while it is being trained on so called labeled data~\cite{MachineLearningGlossary}.

\paragraph{Horizontal Federated Learning} In horizontal federated learning, clients' datasets describe same features meanwhile the data is different~\cite{prayitnoSystematicReviewFederated2021, zhangIntroductionFederatedLearning2022, FederatedLearningWhat, strykerWhatFederatedLearning2025a}. For distinction with vertical federated learning, it is important here to keep in mind an almost obvious fact --- By saying ``the data is different'' between clients, we mean that the feature that uniquely identifies a record, such as an email or a national identification number, is never duplicated across clients' datasets.   For example, there are most likely many hospitals worldwide that have treated sleep apnea and have targeted same medical features, such as heart rate and oxygen saturation, but collected different data for different patients (see Figure~\ref{fig:types-of-federated-learning}). A federated learning model could predict sleep apnea based on all hospitals' client models.

\paragraph{Vertical Federated Learning} In vertical federated learning, clients' datasets describe different data and different features, except for identification features such as identification number~\cite{prayitnoSystematicReviewFederated2021, zhangIntroductionFederatedLearning2022, FederatedLearningWhat, strykerWhatFederatedLearning2025a}. For example, a country has a hospital for sleep disorders that collects features like patient national identification number, heart rate and oxygen saturation, and a cancer hospital that collects patient national identification number, x-ray scan images, blood information (visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:types-of-federated-learning}, although a health insurance is used instead of a cancer hospital)~\cite{shiVerticalFederatedLearning2025}. A federated learning model could connect the knowledge of both hospitals using patient identifier, find patterns, and become more holistic in its predictions.

\paragraph{Federated Transfer Learning} In federated transfer learning, clients' datasets describe different data and different features, including the identification features ~\cite{prayitnoSystematicReviewFederated2021, zhangIntroductionFederatedLearning2022, FederatedLearningWhat, strykerWhatFederatedLearning2025a}. For example, two countries have two hospitals and there is very little overlap between features or data --- the patients' identification is also mostly different --- yet federated transfer learning can be applied in this scenario to combine their knowledge.

We will focus on horizontal federated learning prototype, because our features remain same across clients, namely hospitals.

\subsubsection{Decentralized learning techniques}

\emph{Fog computing} is an improved approach on top of federated learning. It originates from the network challenges of federated learning,  focuses on layered architecture of communication between local and global model, and aims to reduce network traffic~\cite{gorrichoMASTERSDEGREETELECOMMUNICATIONS}.

Being a decentralized technique, federated learning still has a central, global model that aggregates all parameter updates. \emph{Gossip learning} --- also called distributed federated learning (DFL)~\cite{shammarSwarmLearningSurvey2025} --- is even simpler in this sense, as it does not have a global model. The updates and aggregations are all taken care by the local models themselves~\cite{hegedusDecentralizedLearningWorks2021b}.

Similar to gossip learning is \emph{swarm learning}. The main difference is its use of blockchain peer-to-peer communication that is configured between the local models. This enables swarm learning to be a very related solution, with even better potential privacy and fault tolerance (because there is no single point of failure like the global model server)~\cite{shammarSwarmLearningSurvey2025}.

Centralized learning does not come into considerations because the movement of data to a central server is restricted by data regulations. \subsubsection{Privacy preserving techniques} \label{subsubsec:privacy-perserving-techniques}

In general, \emph{homomorphic encryption} creates a possibility to make operations on encrypted data, without ever decrypting it. This can be applied both to normal and federated machine learning, although it is rather an addon than a replacement for the latter. The challenges include computational overhead and lack of support for some operations, thus finding a balance between privacy and performance is an important question~\cite{hallajiDecentralizedFederatedLearning2024, marquesTrainingMachineLearning2026}.

\emph{Secure multiparty computation} allows to split private data from many participants, share it in this split form, and let any participant compute (public) functions, such as average and maximum without knowing any other participants' private data~\cite{evansPragmaticIntroductionSecure}. In federated learning, sharing weights with the central server still brings up some privacy concerns that can be handled by applying secure multiparty computation~\cite{mugunthanSMPAISecureMultiParty}. This adds some computation cost, along with even more communication overhead~\cite{hallajiDecentralizedFederatedLearning2024}.

\emph{Differential privacy} introduces random noise to private data while still maintaining its statistical properties~\cite{dankarPracticingDifferentialPrivacy2013}. This means that individual values like age are uninterpretable for any middleman between global and local models, thus guarding the system from inference and poisoning attacks. For example, inference attack would mean that a middleman can infer whether a person has a certain disease from the outputs of the global model~\cite{wuInferenceAttacksTaxonomy2024}; data poisoning attack would mean that a middleman intercepts and modifies the weights sent to the global or local model, making the predictions of the model less accurate or corrupted~\cite{gabrielliSurveyDecentralizedFederated2023a}.

\subsection{Comparative study}

% Did you conduct a comparative study of suitable technologies? \label{q} \newline
From~\cite{braungardtFlowerPySyftCo2024, yurdemFederatedLearningOverview2024, riedelComparativeAnalysisOpensource2024}, I have extracted four main open-source frameworks suitable for the project. One of them, namely FedML, had vastly different opinions among papers, so I did not take this framework into consideration.

Flower: It's main characteristic is easy integration with different deep learning frameworks, and that it offers a beginner- and prototype-friendly entrance into federated learning~\cite{mehdiComprehensiveReviewOpenSource2025, yurdemFederatedLearningOverview2024, rajeshkumarTop10Federated2026, guptaBenchmarkingFederatedLearning}. As Flower was new in 2025, research papers from that year showed it has lacked community support and documentation~\cite{mehdiComprehensiveReviewOpenSource2025, yurdemFederatedLearningOverview2024}, which has changed the next year, and is now apparently considered to have strong documentation~\cite{rajeshkumarTop10Federated2026}. It is especially well-suited for edge IoT devices and production ready~\cite{mehdiComprehensiveReviewOpenSource2025}, although additional privacy features have to be engineered manually~\cite{rajeshkumarTop10Federated2026}, unlike with frameworks like NVIDIA FLARE\@.

NVIDIA Federated Learning Application Runtime Environment (NVIDIA FLARE): It's main characteristic is enterprise-readiness~\cite{mehdiComprehensiveReviewOpenSource2025, cotocusTop10Federated2026, rajeshkumarTop10Federated2026, guptaBenchmarkingFederatedLearning}, with many built-in features, especially for privacy~\cite{cotocusTop10Federated2026}. It is overall very good in all aspects (also documentation)~\cite{braungardtFlowerPySyftCo2024, mehdiComprehensiveReviewOpenSource2025}, which is the reason it might seem heavy for smaller projects~\cite{rajeshkumarTop10Federated2026}. The main disadvantages is that you are restricted to the NVIDIA ecosystem~\cite{mehdiComprehensiveReviewOpenSource2025}.

TensorFlow Federated (TFF): It's main characteristic is that is most widely used, but complex, only compatible with TensorFlow, and can be integrated into the least environments~\cite{mehdiComprehensiveReviewOpenSource2025, yurdemFederatedLearningOverview2024}.

%Is there a justification for the technologies that were selected? \label{q} \newline
The choice comes down to Flower or NVIDIA FLARE. Luckily, these two have recently announced collaboration and compatibility between each other~\cite{rothSuperchargingFederatedLearning2024, saysSuperchargingFederatedLearning2025, laneAnnouncingNVIDIAFlower2024a}, meaning Flower can be integrated into the NVIDIA runtime environment and immediately leveraging enterprise-ready environment from NVIDIA and simplicity from Flower. For this reason, Flower will be used to create the prototype.
